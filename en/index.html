A reference for origin, variety, and process. Built for coffee lovers who already know what coffee is and just want to look up what they have in their hands and what to do with it.

Section 1 — Reference Section 2 — Filter Standalone Document

This document has two sections. Section 1 is the reference: producing countries and their famous regions, varieties grown around the world, and processing methods. It's organized to be skimmed and looked up rather than read end-to-end. Section 2 is an interactive filter that lets you select a country, variety, and process — including blends across multiple of any of those — and produces a combined summary tailored to what you have in front of you.

What's deliberately left out: history of coffee, equipment guides, brewing methods in detail, growing economics, social context. There are good books and websites for those things. This document does one job — quickly answer the question "I have this bag in my hand, what should I expect and how should I approach it?"

How to read entries quickly

Each origin, variety, and process is presented as a card with structured fields. The fields are always in the same order, so once you've read one card you can navigate any of them. The most important fields for at-the-counter use are Taste image, Roasting suggestion, and Brewing suggestion.

How to read an unfamiliar label

A bag often gives you a region, an altitude, a variety name, and a process name — even when one of those is unfamiliar. Use what you can recognize as context. An "experimental anaerobic from Sidamo at 1,950m" is going to inherit Sidamo's heirloom citric character and high altitude density even if the specific fermentation isn't documented anywhere. A "regional hybrid Castillo selection from Huila" will behave like other Huila Caturra-family Colombians within a small range of variation. The unfamiliar variable narrows the answer; it rarely overrides the known ones.

Two categories at the end of Section 1 — Other / Regional Hybrid / Unidentified Varieties and Experimental / Modern Innovation Processes — exist to handle the things that aren't in the standard list yet. They give you decision rules rather than flavor descriptions, because predicting flavor for something we can't name precisely is dishonest.

A note on accuracy

Coffee is a living industry. Famous farms change ownership, new varieties get released, processing methods evolve year to year. The information here reflects what's broadly established in specialty coffee literature (Hoffmann's World Atlas of Coffee, the World Coffee Research catalog, Mikami's Coffee大図鑑, and others). Where this document and a fresh roaster's bag disagree, trust the bag — the farm itself or the roaster has the most current information about that specific lot.

Section 1 · Reference

Producing Countries & Regions

Twenty-four countries that matter to specialty coffee, grouped by region. Each entry covers the famous growing zones, the varieties commonly grown there, the main processing traditions, the typical taste image, and — where well-documented — notable farms. Farm lists are examples, not complete lists.

Coffee Varieties

Thirty-five varieties that account for the overwhelming majority of specialty coffee, organized by family. The classical lineage runs from Typica and Bourbon, through their natural mutations and crosses, to modern hybrids developed for disease resistance. Ethiopian heirloom varieties — the genetic origin of all the others — sit in their own group because their diversity is too wide for the family-tree model. Three other coffee species (Eugenioides, Robusta, Liberica, Stenophylla) appear in their own group at the end.

Processing Methods

How the cherry becomes a green bean shapes the cup as much as where the coffee grew. The eight standard processes here cover almost any coffee you will encounter. The frontier category at the end exists because new processes are being invented faster than any reference document can keep up with.
